Knowledge Base

Technical reference data, quality logic, and performance terms.

Fiber Quality

Micronaire: A measure tied to fineness and maturity. Extreme values can create processing and hand-feel issues.

Staple Length: Longer fibers usually spin into smoother, stronger, more refined yarns.

Length Uniformity: More even fiber length improves yarn consistency and reduces weak points.

Trash / Leaf Content: Cleaner cotton generally supports better yarn appearance and processing quality.

Yarn Architecture

Ideal Counts: 30 - 40 is a strong range for many premium knits. Higher counts usually feel finer and lighter.

Ply Construction: Multi-ply yarns can improve durability, balance, and dimensional stability.

Yarn Numbering: Count systems help explain why two garments with similar weight can feel very different.

Spinning & Preparation

Carded vs Combed: Combing removes shorter fibers and usually yields a smoother, more refined yarn.

Ring-spun & Compact: Often preferred for smoother and stronger yarn structure.

Vortex: Can improve anti-pilling and strength at the cost of some softness and drape.

Open-End: Faster and cheaper, but often rougher and less refined.

Wear Performance

Abrasion Resistance: How well fabric withstands repeated rubbing and surface wear.

Pilling: Surface fuzz and pills often reflect fiber length, yarn structure, and finishing.

Shrinkage: Construction, finishing, and care all affect dimensional stability after washing.

Repairability: Garments that age well are often easier to mend, re-dye, or recondition.
